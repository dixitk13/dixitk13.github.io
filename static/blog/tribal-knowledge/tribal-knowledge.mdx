## 📒 Background

Before we begin today about tribal knowledge, I'd like to briefly throw in some context because this can have a broad meaning.

The basic LMGTFY (let me google that for you) meaning for tribal knowledge would be unwritten, unspoken, and undocumented
institutional knowledge within an organization held by specific individuals and groups.

## 🦉 Building knowledge

One of the many things large organizations do is build a lot of tribal knowledge base, possibly on a variety of topics
from choosing technological decisions to executing leadership strategies.

One may ask, how does an organization build this so-called tribal knowledge? Historically, based on observations,
much of this aggregates when teams (and organizations) move at unusually faster speeds to achieve goals necessary to
meet crucial timelines, possibly sacrificing aspects necessary for long-term growth.

Why would an organization operate in such a manner, building short-term wins while sacrificing long-term growth?
After all, it doesn't make sense because STCG (short-term capital gain) is taxed fairly higher
than LTCG (long-term capital gain) - so gravitating towards long-term would seem logical.

## 💬 Anecdote

Before proceeding, I want to give an anecdote here, and I'll need to set some background for this one.
My team would organize a small pizza party on Fridays (when WFH wasn't the new norm) just as a
team-building exercise for the organization. Being naive and young, went around the office open
space, trying to take note of everyone's pizza preference, possibly to maximize gratitude.

Little did I realize I was slowly falling into the sandpit of indecision.

My manager came up to me and whispered in my ear, "Don't try to achieve consensus execute a popular choice -immediately!"

I wasn't convinced back then, but eventually executed the "popular decision". After all, it was the exact
opposite of what I was trying to do.

But turns out that it's pretty hard to navigate options at times, and people can have a variety
of it - but I'd suggest folks try this one. The "popular decision" back then was just Garden veggies,
Pepperoni, and cheese - easy!

## 🦥 Moving on

Organizations do something similar at times and often get labeled as "closed organizations" for
this - indicating restricted flow of information. This also brings in weighted choices from the
"trusted inner circle" of members, which most possibly includes management and select ICs.

This helps organizations make faster decisions and brings in bias for action.

## 🌊 Information flow

Information flow that happened behind closed doors may seem like high entropy to the outside universe observing it.

Organically breaking this is a choice leadership involved in this exchange should share
(or perhaps document) - otherwise, this starts becoming tribal knowledge!

Some organizations also choose to flow this information down like a crystal lattice by means of
mentorship and mentor-mentee relationships to ensure continued information flow.

Knowledge also starts contributing to "becoming tribal" when people change teams or when people
start changing companies.

As innocuous as it may seem, the obstructed information flow also hampers the productivity and culture of the organization.
